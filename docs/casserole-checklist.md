# CASSEROLE Checklist — Good Neighbor Protocol

Use this checklist before handing a request to anyone: a teammate, a contractor, a developer, a designer, a collaborator, or yourself.

The goal is not to fill out a form. The goal is to make sure the request can actually travel.

> A casserole is not perfect. It is prepared.

---

## The CASSEROLE Elements

Work through each element. Ask the question. Answer it honestly. If you can't answer it, that's useful information — it means there's a gap to close before the request goes anywhere.

---

### ✦ C — Clarity

> Is the ask understandable? Can another person tell what is being requested without having to guess at the meaning?

- [ ] The core ask is written out in plain language
- [ ] The expected output or deliverable is described
- [ ] There is no language that's vague by habit ("make it better," "just clean it up," "you know what I mean")
- [ ] Someone unfamiliar with the backstory could read this and understand what's needed

*What is being asked?*

---

### ✦ A — Alignment

> Are the people involved solving the same problem, or are they carrying different pictures of what the request is about?

- [ ] The goal has been stated, not assumed
- [ ] The person requesting and the person receiving understand what success looks like
- [ ] This request is consistent with broader priorities — or the deviation is intentional and noted

*What is the goal?*

---

### ✦ S — Scope

> What is actually included in this ask? What is being requested right now?

- [ ] What is included in this request is explicitly described
- [ ] What is excluded (or assumed to be excluded) is noted
- [ ] Any gray areas — things that might be assumed to be in scope but aren't — are addressed

*What is in scope? What is out of scope?*

---

### ✦ S — Safety

> What sensitive stakes are involved? Does this request touch people, data, privacy, access, or situations that require extra care?

- [ ] Any people, privacy concerns, or sensitive data involved are identified
- [ ] Dependencies, integrations, or systems that could be disrupted are noted
- [ ] Anything that has caused problems before and should be treated carefully is flagged

*What must be protected?*

---

### ✦ E — Expectations

> What does success look like? What does "done," "better," or "ready" actually mean in this case?

- [ ] The expected output format is described
- [ ] The quality bar is set — not just "good," but specifically what "good" means here
- [ ] Completion criteria are written out
- [ ] A deadline or timeframe is included, or its absence is intentional and noted

*What does done look like?*

---

### ✦ R — Reasonability

> Is this request realistic, fair, and proportionate to the time, resources, complexity, and phase of the work?

- [ ] The timeline is achievable given real constraints
- [ ] The required resources and access are available or accounted for
- [ ] The ask is within the realistic capability of the person receiving it
- [ ] Nothing has been overlooked that would make this impossible or unfair as written

*What makes this reasonable? What would make it unreasonable?*

---

### ✦ O — Ownership

> Who is responsible for what? Who is asking, who is carrying, who is deciding, and who remains accountable for the truth of the request?

- [ ] The decision-maker is identified
- [ ] The person doing the work is identified
- [ ] Anyone who needs to review or approve is identified
- [ ] Anyone who should be kept in the loop (but not asked to act) is identified

*Who owns what?*

---

### ✦ L — Limits

> What boundaries, constraints, non-goals, or realities need to be named so the work does not quietly expand into something else?

- [ ] Hard deadlines are noted
- [ ] Budget constraints (if any) are stated
- [ ] Tool, access, or platform constraints are noted
- [ ] Legal, compliance, or policy limits that apply are flagged

*What are the non-negotiables?*

---

### ✦ E — Explain-Why

> What reasoning supports this request? Why does this path make sense, and what assumptions are being made along the way?

- [ ] The reason this request exists is stated
- [ ] The problem it solves (or the opportunity it addresses) is described
- [ ] What happens if it isn't done — or isn't done well — is noted
- [ ] Any context that would change how someone approaches the work is included

*Why does this matter?*

---

## Quick-Use Version

In a hurry? Ask yourself these nine questions before sending:

1. **Clarity** — Could someone act on this without asking me anything?
2. **Alignment** — Do we agree on what success looks like?
3. **Scope** — Have I said what's in and what's out?
4. **Safety** — Is anything sensitive, private, or fragile involved?
5. **Expectations** — Have I described what "done" actually means?
6. **Reasonability** — Is this actually doable as I've described it?
7. **Ownership** — Is it clear who is responsible for what?
8. **Limits** — Have I named the real constraints?
9. **Explain-Why** — Have I explained why this matters?

If you can't answer any of these, close the message and do another casserole pass before sending.

---

## Before You Send This to Someone Else

Ask yourself:

- [ ] Would I be comfortable receiving this request myself?
- [ ] Does the person receiving this have everything they need to start?
- [ ] Have I named the sensitive stakes, not hidden them?
- [ ] Am I asking for something reasonable given their capacity?
- [ ] Have I explained why this matters, not just what I want?
- [ ] If something goes sideways, is it clear who is accountable?

If any of these is a "no" or a "not sure" — this request needs another casserole pass.

---

## Before You Say Yes to Building This

This version is for the receiving side — the developer, designer, project manager, builder, or team steward reading a request someone has handed you.

Before you say yes and begin:

- [ ] Do I understand what is actually being asked?
- [ ] Do I know what "done" means for this person?
- [ ] Is the scope clear enough that I won't be guessing at the edges?
- [ ] Are there privacy, safety, or access concerns I need to flag?
- [ ] Is this realistic in the timeline and with the resources available?
- [ ] Do I know who I report to if I hit a decision point?
- [ ] Are there constraints I need to name before I begin?
- [ ] Do I understand why this matters — and does that affect how I'd approach it?

If several of these are unclear, it is appropriate to ask for another casserole pass before starting. You are not being difficult. You are protecting your time and the quality of the work.
