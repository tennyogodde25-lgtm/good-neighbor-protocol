# The Commandments — Good Neighbor Protocol

These are the ten commandments of the Good Neighbor Protocol.

They are not meant to sound holy, rigid, or self-righteous. They are not there to shame people for being human. They are there to give collaboration a backbone — the short-form truths that hold the longer protocol together.

If the core protocol is the casserole dish, the commandments are the kitchen rules that keep people from setting the stove on fire. They are reminders of what good handoffs require. They are warnings about what chaos tends to look like in disguise.

---

## Commandment 1: Bring Structure, Not Vibes

Having a feeling is not the same thing as having a brief.

You are allowed to feel excited. You are allowed to feel urgency. You are allowed to feel that something is off, missing, broken, clunky, or full of potential. But feelings alone are not handoff-ready.

> "Make it pop." / "It just needs more energy." / "I want it to kind of flow better." / "It needs something."

Those statements may be real, but they are not yet usable.

This commandment does not say you cannot start with instinct. It says instinct is the beginning, not the final package. Bring the vibe — but also bring what the problem is, who it affects, what outcome you want, and what "better" actually looks like.

Vibes may start the fire. Structure is what keeps dinner from burning.

---

## Commandment 2: Separate Vision from Execution

A lot of conflict happens because people confuse the dream with the implementation.

The vision is what you are trying to achieve. The execution is how that thing gets built. Those are connected, but they are not the same.

"I want a dashboard" may not actually mean a dashboard. It may mean: I want fewer status questions. I want visibility. I want one place to check what is happening. Those are vision-level needs. Once those are named, a builder can help decide whether the answer is a dashboard, a portal, a report, an email digest, or something simpler.

This commandment protects both sides. It keeps the requester from over-prescribing the solution before the problem is clear. And it keeps the builder from dismissing the deeper intention behind a rough ask.

Name the dream. Then work out the build.

---

## Commandment 3: Define What "Done" Means

A request without a finish line is a trap.

If nobody knows what success looks like, then the work can keep changing forever. This is how people end up stuck in revision loops, endless polishing, quiet resentment, and the miserable sentence: *"It's close, but not quite."*

This commandment asks people to do something very simple and very important: describe the outcome in a way another person can aim at. Not everything needs perfect metrics. But everything needs some clearer destination than "better."

Done does not have to be fancy. It just has to exist. Because if "done" is not defined, then the work never really has permission to land.

---

## Commandment 4: Don't Move the Goalposts Mid-Build

This one causes an enormous amount of hidden damage.

Work begins. Choices get made. Then halfway through, the requester changes the meaning of the request without clearly acknowledging that the target has moved. Silent goalpost movement makes builders feel like they are chasing smoke. It makes timelines useless. It makes revision feel like punishment.

This commandment does not forbid change. Change is normal. Refinement is normal. Discovery is normal. What it forbids is pretending the change is minor when it is actually structural.

If the goal changes, name it. If the scope changes, name it. If the success criteria change, name it. That is not failure. That is honesty. And honesty is much easier to work with than surprise.

---

## Commandment 5: Name the Risk Before the Risk Names You

The easiest risks to ignore are the ones hiding inside ordinary language.

> "Just add access." / "Just collect names." / "Just build a portal." / "Just track the information."

The word "just" has covered up many bad decisions. This commandment asks people to pause long enough to ask: what could go wrong here? Who could be affected if we mishandle this? What kind of data, access, or consequences are attached to this request?

Risk does not only mean dramatic disaster. It can mean privacy concerns, unclear permissions, sensitive information, or features that affect minors, money, safety, or legal exposure.

If a request touches something with real stakes, the stakes need names. Because unnamed risk tends to come back later wearing a much louder outfit. Naming risk early is not negativity. It is care.

---

## Commandment 6: Do Not Make the Builder Translate Fog

When a request is vague, scattered, contradictory, or emotionally overloaded, the builder often becomes the translator by default. They have to figure out what matters, what is optional, what the requester really means, what problem is hiding underneath the proposed solution, and what will probably cause conflict later.

That is a tremendous amount of unpaid interpretation.

Being competent is not consent to becoming the full-time translator for other people's unfinished thinking. This commandment is not anti-builder and not anti-requester. It protects both. The burden of meaning should not be silently dumped onto the most responsible person in the room every single time.

If the request is foggy, the answer is not to demand mind reading. The answer is to shape the handoff.

---

## Commandment 7: Clarify First, Accelerate Second

A lot of chaos is born from the worship of speed.

People skip clarification because it feels slow. But unclear speed is often just delay wearing running shoes. You can move quickly in the wrong direction. You can build fast on a broken premise. You can launch on time and still create a mess that costs more to repair later.

This commandment does not reject speed. It puts speed in the right order.

First clarify. Then accelerate. First identify the problem. Then move. First define the outcome. Then move. First name the risk. Then move.

The goal is not slowness. The goal is clean momentum.

---

## Commandment 8: Leave a Trail, Not a Mystery

One of the most painful things in collaboration is trying to reconstruct a decision after the fact.

*Why was this approved? Why did we choose this path? Why did nobody escalate that concern?*

If there is no trail, people are left with memory, guesswork, and blame. That is why GNP includes Explain-Why thinking as a core principle.

A trail does not have to be dramatic. It just has to be honest. Leave notes. Name assumptions. Record decisions. Say why a path was chosen. Say why a risk was accepted.

Mysteries make teams brittle. Trails make teams resilient. A trail gives people something real to return to instead of "I guess this happened for some reason."

---

## Commandment 9: Respect the Human on the Other Side of the Handoff

Every request lands in a human life.

It lands on someone's calendar. Someone's brain. Someone's emotional bandwidth. Someone's limited time. Someone's expertise. Someone's body, energy, and patience.

To respect the human on the other side of the handoff means asking: Have I made this understandable? Have I given enough context? Have I named the risks? Have I made silent assumptions visible? Have I treated this person like a collaborator or like a cleanup crew?

Respect does not always sound soft. Sometimes respect sounds like structure. Sometimes respect sounds like naming limits. Sometimes respect sounds like slowing down enough not to create preventable pain.

The point is not to be overly delicate. The point is to remember that collaboration is not machine-to-machine. It is person-to-person.

---

## Commandment 10: Good Neighbors Bring Casseroles, Not Chaos

This is the commandment that holds all the others together.

A casserole is not perfect. A casserole is prepared. That is the difference.

To bring a casserole means you did some work before handing it off. You organized what you could. You labeled what mattered. You thought about the other person receiving it. You made it easier to pick up, carry, and act on.

To bring chaos means you handed over fragments and expected someone else to cook the whole meal while you stood nearby saying, "That's not quite what I meant."

This commandment is not there to shame anyone. It is there to remind us what good collaboration feels like.

It feels considered. It feels carryable. It feels structured enough to build on. It feels honest about what is known and what is not. It feels respectful of time, labor, skill, and uncertainty.

Good neighbors bring casseroles, not chaos. That is not just a slogan. It is the ethic underneath the protocol.

---

## Living the Commandments

The commandments are not separate from the core protocol. They are how the protocol stays alive in ordinary moments.

When someone starts vibe-dumping, the commandments remind you to bring structure. When a solution is being proposed too early, they remind you to separate vision from execution. When revisions keep circling, they remind you to define done. When the request starts changing in silence, they remind you not to move the goalposts. When stakes are hiding under ordinary language, they remind you to name the risk. When urgency starts trampling clarity, they remind you to clarify first. When decisions start disappearing into memory, they remind you to leave a trail. When frustration begins making people careless, they remind you to respect the human on the other side.

And when the whole process starts collapsing into confusion, the commandments bring you back to the central image:

*Casseroles, not chaos.*
